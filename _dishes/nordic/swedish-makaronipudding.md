ingredients:
  pasta:
    - "400g elbow macaroni"
    - "Salt for pasta water"
  filling:
    - "250g bacon (fläsk), diced"
    - "2 large leeks, sliced"
    - "1 tablespoon butter"
  custard:
    - "4 large eggs"
    - "600ml whole milk"
    - "200ml heavy cream"
    - "½ teaspoon nutmeg"
    - "½ teaspoon white pepper"
    - "1 teaspoon salt"
  topping:
    - "100g grated cheese (Swedish Västerbotten or mature cheddar)"
    - "2 tablespoons breadcrumbs"
    - "Butter for dotting"

instructions:
  - section: "Cook the Pasta"
    steps:
      - "Cook macaroni in salted water until just al dente (it will continue cooking in oven)."
      - "Drain and set aside."
  - section: "Prepare the Filling"
    steps:
      - "Fry bacon in a large pan until crispy. Remove and drain on paper towels."
      - "In the bacon fat plus butter, sauté leeks until soft and slightly golden, 8-10 minutes."
      - "Season with salt and pepper."
  - section: "Make the Custard"
    steps:
      - "Whisk eggs, milk, and cream together in a large bowl."
      - "Season with nutmeg, white pepper, and salt."
  - section: "Assemble"
    steps:
      - "Preheat oven to 400°F/200°C."
      - "Butter a 9x13 inch baking dish generously."
      - "Combine pasta, bacon, and leeks in the dish."
      - "Pour custard mixture over the pasta, ensuring it's evenly distributed."
      - "Top with grated cheese and breadcrumbs."
      - "Dot with small pieces of butter."
  - section: "Bake"
    steps:
      - "Bake 35-40 minutes until custard is set and top is golden brown."
      - "The center should be just slightly jiggly—it will set as it rests."
      - "Let rest 5-10 minutes before serving."

tips:
  - "Swedish custard (äggstanning) is NOT cheese sauce—this is the key distinction from American mac and cheese"
  - "Västerbotten cheese is traditional but hard to find outside Sweden—use aged cheddar or Gruyère"
  - "The custard should be just set, not rubbery—watch carefully in the final minutes"
  - "White pepper is traditional in Swedish cooking"

history: |
  Makaronipudding has been a staple of Swedish home cooking since the early 20th century, when
  pasta became widely available in Scandinavia. Unlike Mediterranean pasta dishes, Swedish
  versions developed around the custard-binding technique (äggstanning) rather than cheese sauces.

  The dish reflects Sweden's practical approach to cooking—using inexpensive ingredients to create
  satisfying, nutritious meals. The bacon and leek combination is quintessentially Swedish, while
  the custard base connects it to other Scandinavian "puddings" like Janssons Frestelse (Jansson's
  Temptation) made with potatoes and anchovies.

  Today, makaronipudding remains a beloved weeknight dinner, particularly during the dark winter
  months when hearty, warming dishes are essential.

serving_traditions: |
  In Sweden, makaronipudding is ALWAYS served with two essential condiments:
  - **Lingonsylt** (lingonberry jam) - The sweet-tart jam is mandatory
  - **Ketchup** - Yes, ketchup! This is not optional in Swedish tradition

  This combination of savory pasta with sweet jam might seem unusual, but it's beloved by Swedes
  and reflects the Scandinavian love of pairing meats with fruit preserves.

serving_suggestions:
  - "Serve with lingonberry jam—this is non-negotiable in Sweden"
  - "Provide ketchup at the table (Swedish tradition)"
  - "Accompany with a simple green salad"

make_ahead: |
  Can be assembled up to 24 hours ahead before baking. The custard actually improves as it soaks
  into the pasta. Add 10-15 minutes to baking time if refrigerated. Leftovers reheat well at
  350°F covered with foil for 20 minutes.
